Key principles and tradeoffs here:

1. Merit is multidimensional  
A higher SAT score may indicate stronger measured test performance, but admissions committees usually evaluate more than raw scores: coursework, grades in context, initiative, obstacles navigated, recommendations, intellectual vitality, and likely contribution to the cohort.

2. Achievement should be interpreted in context  
Candidate 1’s score was earned with substantial advantages; Candidate 2’s was earned with fewer institutional and financial supports while working part-time. Context does not erase differences in performance, but it affects how performance is interpreted.

3. Readiness versus ranking  
You’ve said both applicants are above the threshold at which students typically persist and graduate. That matters because once both are credibly capable of succeeding, the committee is no longer deciding only who is “qualified enough,” but which form of excellence or promise it most wants to reward.

4. Past accomplishment versus future potential  
Candidate 1 may look stronger on conventional metrics today. Candidate 2 may show stronger evidence of resilience, upward trajectory, and underdeveloped potential due to fewer opportunities. Admissions often involves deciding how much weight to give realized performance versus capacity that may flourish with access.

5. Equity and fairness can point in different directions  
One view of fairness emphasizes selecting the applicant with the strongest measurable academic indicators. Another emphasizes accounting for unequal access to preparation and rewarding accomplishment relative to opportunity. Neither frame is trivial; the tension between them is central here.

6. Institutional mission matters  
If the honors cohort is meant not only to enroll top scorers but also to cultivate talent, expand opportunity, and build a diverse intellectual community, that may support a broader conception of merit. If it is narrowly designed for students with the very highest current academic preparation, that may push toward conventional academic metrics.

7. Small-cohort effects  
Because this is the last seat in a small honors cohort, the decision may also involve cohort composition: who will benefit most from and contribute most to that environment. That is a legitimate consideration if applied consistently and in line with policy.

8. Risk and uncertainty  
Even with historical data, admissions decisions are probabilistic. A score gap may or may not translate into meaningful differences in honors-level performance, especially once both students are above the readiness floor. The question is whether the additional score difference is actually decision-relevant in this context.

So the core tradeoff is not simply “higher score versus hardship.” It is how your committee defines merit when both applicants appear capable of success

Here are the main principles and tradeoffs to weigh in a cafe supplier decision like this:

1. Product quality and consistency  
   The first question is whether the coffee is good enough, and stays that way week after week. A blind taste test helps with current quality, but consistency over time matters just as much.

2. Reliability vs. disruption risk  
   Coffee supply is operationally critical. Even a slightly cheaper or “acceptable” alternative may not be worth it if it increases the chance of delivery issues, stockouts, quality drift, or slower problem resolution.

3. Total value, not just price  
   The real comparison is not only bean cost. It includes delivery terms, minimums, payment terms, support, responsiveness, training, equipment help, and how much management attention the relationship requires.

4. Proven relationship vs. new-option flexibility  
   Your current roaster has earned trust over years, which has real value. The larger distributor may offer scale, negotiating leverage, or backup options. The tradeoff is between the security of a known partner and the flexibility or economics of a broader supplier base.

5. Customer experience and brand fit  
   If customers already like the coffee, changing suppliers creates some risk even if staff find the alternative acceptable. There is also a brand question: does “local roaster” matter to your cafe’s identity, story, or customer loyalty?

6. Short-term gain vs. long-term partnership  
   Switching for modest immediate benefits can weaken a strong long-term relationship. On the other hand, staying purely out of habit can cause you to miss better terms or reduce your bargaining power over time.

7. Optionality and concentration risk  
   Relying on one supplier creates dependency
